Overview

Everglades National Park, established in 1947, spans approximately 1.5 million acres of wetlands, marshes, and mangroves in southern Florida. It preserves the largest subtropical wilderness in North America and is the only U.S. site recognized as a World Heritage Site, Biosphere Reserve, and Wetland of International Importance. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The park supports an extraordinary mosaic of ecosystemsâ€”from sawgrass marshes and hardwood hammocks to pine rocklands and coastal mangrove forests. Wildlife Highlights

Visitors can encounter diverse wildlife, including alligators, crocodiles, wood storks, roseate spoonbills, the elusive Florida panther, and West Indian manatees. Over 350 bird species and numerous reptiles and mammals call the Everglades home. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Conservation Challenges

The Everglades faces threats from water flow diversion, invasive species (like Burmese pythons and melaleuca), and climate-driven challenges such as sea-level rise and saltwater intrusion. Conservation efforts focus on restoring natural flow regimes, habitat management, and species protection. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
